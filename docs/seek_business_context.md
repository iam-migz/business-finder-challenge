What SEEK Business is

SEEK Business is Australia’s business-for-sale marketplace. It is not mainly a jobs site, even though it sits under the broader SEEK brand. Its core purpose is to connect people who want to buy a business, buy into a franchise, or explore self-employment with sellers, brokers, franchisors, licensors, and distributors.

The site describes itself as Australia’s largest online business-for-sale marketplace and says it helps people “realise their dream of owning a business” by connecting them with business and franchise sellers. SEEK Business says it began in 2006 and usually has “over 18,000 opportunities advertised,” although the live search page I checked showed 16,724 businesses for sale, so the exact count changes over time.

In simple terms: it is like a real estate marketplace, but for businesses and franchises instead of houses.

Origin and ownership

SEEK Business is part of the SEEK group. SEEK itself was founded in Melbourne in 1997, grew into a major online employment marketplace company, operates across Australia, New Zealand, and parts of Asia, and is ASX-listed.

SEEK Business specifically started in 2006 as an extension of SEEK’s broader career/livelihood mission. Instead of only helping people find jobs, it targets people who want a different path: becoming their own boss, buying a small business, investing in a franchise, or changing lifestyle/career direction through business ownership.

The legal operator shown in the terms is SEEK Business Pty Ltd, and the privacy policy describes SEEK Business as being within the SEEK Limited group.

What niche it serves

The site is focused on business acquisition and franchise discovery. Its niche is not broad ecommerce and not employment. It is specifically about:

Existing businesses for sale
New franchise opportunities
Franchise resale opportunities
Business brokers and broker portfolios
Franchisor lead generation
Private owner-to-buyer business sales
Licensing, distribution, and self-employment opportunities

The search interface supports searching by keyword, industry, location, radius, and online-business availability. It also lets users filter by business type, investment range, and listing freshness, then sort by business name, date, investment, or closest location.

Who uses SEEK Business?
1. Buyers / aspiring business owners

These are people looking to buy an existing business, buy a franchise, invest in a semi-managed business, or change careers into self-employment. SEEK Business explicitly frames itself around people who want to be their “own boss,” change lifestyle, increase income potential, or shift career direction.

A typical buyer might be:

Someone leaving employment and wanting to own a cafe, cleaning business, gym, retail shop, or service business.
An investor looking for a business with recurring revenue.
A migrant or family buyer looking for a small business.
A professional looking for a franchise model instead of starting from zero.
A buyer comparing business opportunities by location, asking price, category, and support offered.
2. Private business sellers

Private sellers can advertise their own businesses directly. SEEK Business has seller packages, including a free option and paid packages such as Standard and Accelerate, with paid tiers offering more visibility, longer listing support, photos, recommendation emails, or priority placement. The seller FAQ says owners can sell directly and that SEEK Business does not charge commission.

3. Business brokers

Business brokers use the site to advertise multiple businesses and build a broker profile. SEEK Business has a broker directory, and broker profiles can show broker name, location, logo, number of opportunities, specialties, serviced locations, consultants, testimonials, and website links.

For brokers, SEEK Business is basically a lead generation channel.

4. Franchisors

Franchisors use the platform to recruit franchisees. SEEK Business has dedicated franchisor advertising products, including franchise ads, profile pages, banners, sponsorships, offsite advertising, and performance reporting.

So for franchisors, the product is not just a listing. It is a franchise recruitment funnel.

5. Licensors and distributors

The official About page says opportunities are advertised not only by business owners, brokers, and franchisors, but also by licensors and distributors.

This means the marketplace can include opportunities that are not traditional “buy this existing business” deals, but also licensing/distribution models.

How the site makes money

The business model appears to be mostly advertising and lead generation.

Private sellers can pay for better listing visibility. Brokers can use casual ads, subscriptions, boosted ads, email boosts, social boosts, display ads, and broker profile products. Franchisors can buy franchise advertising packages, branded pages, banners, sponsorships, and offsite campaigns.

So the platform has two sides:

Buyers browse for free and submit enquiries.
Sellers, brokers, and franchisors pay to reach those buyers.

This is very similar to marketplaces like realestate.com.au or Carsales, except the asset being sold is a business or franchise opportunity.

What useful public data can be extracted

Important distinction: technically visible data is not the same as legally/contractually allowed data. SEEK Business terms say the site is for personal purposes and restrict copying, collecting databases, storing significant portions, or commercially exploiting site content without authorization.

So for a serious/commercial scraper, you should treat this as: request permission, use a data agreement, or build only a small internal research crawler that respects robots.txt and terms.

With that caveat, here is the useful data visible from public pages.

1. Listing search result data

Search result pages expose useful structured marketplace data. From result cards, you can collect:

Data field	Why useful
Listing title	Identifies the opportunity.
Listing URL / ID	Stable key for tracking listings over time.
Advertiser / brand / broker name	Useful for broker/franchise network analysis.
Location	Enables suburb, city, state, and regional heatmaps.
Asking price / investment amount	Useful for pricing benchmarks.
Price qualifiers	Examples: P.O.A., negotiable, plus SAV, etc.
Listing type	Existing business, new franchise, franchise resale, etc.
Industry and subcategory	Useful for niche-level analysis.
Short description/snippet	Good for keyword extraction and AI classification.
Date/listing freshness	Helps estimate churn and time-on-market.
Featured/promoted status	Indicates paid promotion or higher advertiser intent.

The public search pages show title, advertiser/brand, location, price, type, snippet, and industry hierarchy in the listing cards.

2. Listing detail page data

The detail pages are richer. A listing can include:

Data field	Example use
Full title	Clean opportunity name.
Date posted	Freshness and listing age.
Location hierarchy	State/city/suburb analysis.
Investment level	Valuation and price range analysis.
Business type	Franchise/new/resale/existing business.
Industry hierarchy	Category classification.
Summary	AI summarization and opportunity scoring.
Location details	Geospatial matching.
About the opportunity	Business model extraction.
Marketing support	Franchise support comparison.
Training provided	Franchise onboarding quality signal.
Skills required	Buyer-fit analysis.
Year established	Business maturity signal.
Site locations	Franchise footprint.
History / awards	Brand credibility signal.
Images/videos	Visual classification, branding, media availability.
Related listings	Advertiser network mapping.

One franchise listing page, for example, includes posted date, location, investment level, type, industry, enquiry form, contact area, media, summary, location details, marketing support, training, skills, year established, site locations, history, awards, and more listings from the same advertiser.

Contact names and click-to-reveal phone numbers can appear on listings, but those should be treated carefully as personal/contact data and should not be harvested at scale without permission.

3. Franchise brand data

The franchise directory is especially valuable because it groups opportunities by brand. You can extract:

Data field	Why useful
Franchise brand name	Brand-level database.
Category	Industry segmentation.
Locations available	Territory expansion mapping.
Brand logo	Visual identity.
Short description	Brand positioning.
External website/social links	Enrichment.
Number of franchisees / years operating	Brand maturity signal, where shown.
Available opportunities	Current expansion activity.
Investment price by location	Franchise cost benchmarking.
Franchise journey/enquiry process	Sales funnel analysis.

The directory exposes A–Z franchise brands, category/location labels, descriptions, and brand pages. Individual franchise pages can include about sections, external links, brand benefits, featured opportunities, price, type, and enquiry process details.

This is probably one of the most useful datasets if your goal is franchise market intelligence.

4. Broker directory data

The broker directory and broker profiles can be used to build a database of business brokers. Useful fields include:

Data field	Use case
Broker name	Broker directory.
Head office / address	Geographic coverage.
Number of opportunities	Broker activity level.
Specialties	Industry expertise.
Locations serviced	Service area mapping.
Consultant names	Team mapping, but handle as personal data.
Website	Enrichment.
Testimonials	Reputation signals.
Broker descriptions	Positioning and specialization.

The broker pages expose broker summaries, locations, opportunities, specialties, serviced areas, consultants, and testimonials.

Again, consultant names and direct contact information should be handled carefully.

5. Category and location market data

This is one of the highest-value scraping targets because it can produce market intelligence over time.

You can track:

Number of businesses for sale by category.
Number of businesses for sale by city/state/suburb.
Median asking price by category.
Price range by niche.
Franchise vs independent business mix.
New listings per week.
Removed listings per week.
Listing churn.
Approximate time-on-market.
Broker share of listings.
Brand/franchise expansion by region.
Overcrowded categories.
Underserved categories.

For example, the site exposes location-specific result pages such as Melbourne, where the page showed 3,901 businesses for sale in Melbourne - Greater at the time checked.

This type of data is useful for:

Franchise competitor analysis.
Market entry research.
Business valuation comparables.
Broker lead generation.
Investment opportunity discovery.
Regional demand/supply mapping.
6. Advice/content library data

SEEK Business also has an advice section with buying and selling guides, finance articles, checklists, and business ownership content.

Scrapeable content metadata could include:

Data field	Use
Article title	Content index.
Category	Topic clustering.
Read time	Content depth estimate.
Publish/update date, if visible	Freshness.
Article URL	Reference link.
Main topic	Buyer education taxonomy.

This is less valuable than listing data, but useful if you are building a content/research assistant around buying businesses.

7. Internal platform data you cannot directly scrape

SEEK Business likely has much more valuable internal data than what is visible publicly. Its own broker advertising page mentions marketplace insights based on marketplace interactions, including industries, locations, advertised prices, and enquiry volumes. It also mentions that enquiries rose 16% in 2023 and continued growing in 2024.

The privacy policy also references aggregate data such as page views, visits, searches, enquiries, time on site, search criteria, and entry/exit paths.

That means the highest-value internal data would be:

Buyer search demand.
Enquiry conversion rates.
Views per listing.
Saved listings.
Buyer budgets.
Buyer locations.
Industry demand by region.
Lead quality.
Broker/franchisor campaign performance.

But that is not public scrapeable data. You would need a partnership, report, account access, or commercial data arrangement.

Best data products you could build from SEEK Business data

If you were analyzing this site for a project, these would be the most useful outputs:

1. Business-for-sale market dashboard

Track listings by category, location, price band, and listing type.

Useful for answering:

Which business categories have the most supply?
Which regions have the most opportunities?
What is the average asking price for cafes in Melbourne?
Are cleaning businesses cheaper than food businesses?
Which suburbs have high business-sale activity?
2. Franchise opportunity intelligence

Track franchise brands, investment ranges, expansion regions, support offered, and number of opportunities.

Useful for:

Comparing franchises.
Detecting fast-expanding brands.
Finding lower-cost franchise opportunities.
Mapping franchise territory availability.
3. Broker activity tracker

Track which brokers list the most businesses, in what industries, and in which regions.

Useful for:

Finding active brokers.
Lead generation.
Market share analysis.
Broker specialization mapping.
4. Valuation comparables dataset

Use asking prices, industry, location, and business type to estimate market ranges.

Useful for:

Business buyers checking whether a listing is overpriced.
Sellers benchmarking their asking price.
Brokers doing rough comps.
Investors screening categories.

Caveat: asking price is not sale price, and many listings use P.O.A. or incomplete price data, so this should be treated as indicative only.

5. Listing churn and time-on-market tracker

By crawling periodically, you can detect:

New listings.
Removed listings.
Price changes.
Description changes.
Listings that keep reappearing.
Categories with fast turnover.

This can become a proxy for demand, although it will not perfectly equal actual sales.
